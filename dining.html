To start off

The UC San Diego Mobile App is a location-based mobile application that features: Classes and Schedules, Location Based Shuttle Arrivals, Dining Menus and Information, Real-time Parking Info, Current Events, etc.

The UC San Diego Mobile App is utilize daily by thousands of students and faculty.

Design Goal

For this project, we decided to focus on redesigning the "dining meus and information" section, which is poorly organized. Our goal is to redesign the dining section's interface and improve the navigation flow.

"How can we refine the information platform of to improve the navigation and readability."

Research

User Interview

In order to understand a user‚Äôs interaction and experience with the dining section of the UCSD app, we conducted interviews and asked interviewees to perform specific tasks using the app. We asked the interviewees questions before, during, and after performing the tasks to understand their familiarity of the application. The interviewees of this study are derived from a random sample of students from UCSD and adults from La Jolla. 13/18 of the participants were students (72.2%), and 5/18 of the participants were randomly selected La Jolla residents (27.7%).

Interview Methodology

  • Random sampling of people at UCSD and off-campus
  • Used the master-apprentice model to understand their eating habits
  • As they were doing the tasks, we questioned the interviewees about the choices they made in order to understand their reasoning
  • Screen recorded the participants‚Äô performing

Problems and Trends

The users helped us to identify the core problems of the UCSD App‚Äôs Dining Sections during their interviews and tasks. Their favored method of choice of letting us know these was by verbally complaining about the app itself. We went in further through tasks and a few more ‚Äúwhy‚Äôs‚Äù to pinpoint their exact grievances felt when navigating the the Dining Section.


Hard to Navigate
  • No search bar: A lot of work, tedious to scroll endlessly to look for what someone wants
  • No filtering: Cannot filter the search
"There was no way to see the distinctions between dining halls and PC restaurants at a quick glance, so I felt frustrated that there wasn't a simple search tool." (User 15)

Disorganized
  • Unordered: No apparent order, information is scattered (e.g. not ordered alphabetically or by distance)
  • Not categorized: No visual difference between dining hall, market, restaurant, coffee place or something else
‚ÄúSo disorganized. Doesn't make me want to use this app because it's hard to use. I hate that I have to keep scrolling, there's no search bar, nothing to filter what I'm looking for‚Äù (User 4). ‚ÄúIt is pretty useless. There is no filtering, it needs search bar. . . disorganized!‚Äù (User 5).

Lack of Differentiation
  • No comparison on the prices: Impossible to determine what‚Äôs cheap and expensive
  • Users unsure of what type of food is served at each establishment
‚Äú. . .it's a long list and there is no order, should have separate dining halls and restaurants‚Äù (User 18)

Although the UC San Diego mobile app does afford users the ability to search through the application to answer all their questions, it is not the most realistic or efficient way of providing information preoccupied students or faculty. The app had become more of an inconvenience than a daily tool to be utilized. These trends kept repeating themselves, in our data and in the visible frustrations of our users.

Before completing the interviews ‚Äúin the real world,‚Äù we anticipated that our primary problems would center around a lack of information or restaurant details. However, the core problems as revealed by the users were more deeply rooted in the uncertainty they faced when overwhelmed with too many options. As discussed later in our eventual re-design, we forgoed making the most viable information available for practical and eye-catching ways of presenting information such as payment methods and hours.

Process

Ideation

Following the double diamond design, we analyzed the problem and redesigned the interface. For the solution, we brainstormed our ideas and sketched as many prototypes we could come up with. Since we all had different ideas, each of us drew the prototype we had in mind, and then combined our idea into the best version. The sketches helped us come to an agreement on the redesign.
